\section{Problem 1}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item % a)
    We begin by proving the hint. Firstly we show that under ELE
    $\E g^n_t = \frac {Z_{\beta_t}}{Z_{\beta_{t-1}}}$
    By the ELE assumption we can replace expectations in
    $g(X_{t-1})$ with expectations over $X_{t-1}$ \emph{even if $X_{t-1}$ is not
    actually distributed according to $\pi_{\beta_{t-1}}$}.
    \begin{align}
        \E g^n_t &= \E_{\pi_{\beta_{t-1}}}
            \frac{\gamma_{\beta_{t}}(X_{t-1})}{\gamma_{\beta_{t-1}}(X_{t-1})} \\
        &= \int \pi_{\beta_{t-1}}(\d x_{t-1})
            \frac{\gamma_{\beta_{t}}(x_{t-1})}{\gamma_{\beta_{t-1}}(x_{t-1})} \\
        &= \int \pi_{\beta_{t-1}}(\d x_{t-1})
            \frac{\pi_{\beta_{t}}(x_{t-1}) Z_t}{\pi_{\beta_{t-1}}(x_{t-1}) Z_{t-1}} \\ 
        &= \frac{Z_t}{Z_{t-1}} 
    \end{align}
    As in the question defintion, set $z_t := \frac{Z_t}{Z_{t-1}}$. 
    Therefore the normalized weights have expectation $1$.
    We now consider the \emph{variance} of the normalized weights $\frac{g_n^t}{z_t}$.
    Playing the same trick with expectations under ELE we get
    \begin{align}
        \Var g^n_t &= \E \left[ \left(\frac{g^n_t}{z_t} - 1\right)^2 \right] \\
        &= \E_{\pi_{\beta_{t-1}}}
        \left[ \left(\frac{\gamma_{\beta_{t}}(X_{t-1})/Z_t}
        {\gamma_{\beta_{t-1}}(X_{t-1})/Z_{t-1}} - 1\right)^2 \right] \\
        &=  \E_{\pi_{\beta_{t-1}}}
        \left[ \left(\frac{\pi_{\beta_{t}}(X_{t-1})}
        {\pi_{\beta_{t-1}}(X_{t-1})} - 1\right)^2 \right] \\
        &= \chi^2(\pi_{\beta_t} \| \pi_{\beta_{t-1}})
    \end{align}

    \newcommand{\AIS}{{\text{AIS}}}

    Now that the hint is proven, we may tackle the variance of the estimators.
    We begin with AIS. Each particle is independent, so we just have to consider
    a single particle going from start to finish. We denote this particles
    estimate of the normalizing constant $\hat Z^{n}_\AIS$. We begin by showing that the
    AIS estimator is unbiased. We use ELE to get independence and split
    up the expectation of the product as a product of expectations. This leaves
    a telescoping product which can be reduced in terms of its first and
    last terms.

    \begin{align}
        \E \hat Z^n_{\AIS} &= 
            \E \prod_{t=1}^T g^n_T \\
        &= \prod_{t=1}^T \E g^n_T \\
        &= \prod_{t=1}^T \frac{Z_T}{Z_{T-1}} \\
        &= \frac{Z_T}{Z_0}
    \end{align}
    Assuming the reference is normalized, and that $Z_T := Z$ as in the
    question, we see that for each particle the estimator $\hat Z^n_{\AIS}$
    is unbiased for $Z$. As an arithmetic mean of unbiased estimators, the overall 
    estimator $\hat Z_\AIS$ is also unbiased.
    
    We now tackle the variance, again
    independence lets us break up products of expectations as expectations
    of products. Further breaking up $Z = \prod_{t=1}^T z_t$ we get
    \begin{align}
        \E \left(\frac{{\hat Z}^{n}_\AIS}{Z}\right)^2 &=
            \E \left(\prod_{t=1}^T \frac{g^n_t}{z_t} \right)^2 \\
            &= \prod_{t=1}^T \E\left({\frac{g_t^{n}}{z_t}}\right)^2 \\
            &= \prod_{t=1}^T \left(\Var \frac{g^n_t}{z_t} + \left(\E \frac{g^n_t}{z_t}\right)^2\right)\\
            &= \prod_{t=1}^T \left(\chi^2(\pi_{\beta_t}\| \pi_{\beta_{t-1}}) + 1 \right)
    \end{align}
    Recognizing that each particle's path is independent, and letting
    $D_t = \log\left(1+\chi^2(\pi_{\beta_t}\|\pi_{\beta_{t-1}})\right)$ we
    see that the variance of the overall estimator is given by 
    \begin{align}
        \Var \left(\frac{{\hat Z}_\AIS}{Z}\right) &= 
            \frac 1 N \Var\left[\frac{\hat Z^{n}_\AIS}{Z}\right] \\
            &= \frac 1 N \left[ \E \left[\left(\frac{\hat Z^{n}_\AIS}{Z}\right)^2\right] 
                - \E \left[ \frac{\hat Z^{n}_\AIS}{Z}\right]^2 \right] \\
            &= 
           \frac{\prod_{t=1}^T \left(\chi^2(\pi_{\beta_t}\| \pi_{\beta_{t-1}}) + 1 \right) - 1}{N}  \\
           &= \frac{\exp\left(\sum_{t=1}^T D_t\right) - 1}{N}
    \end{align}

            \frac 1 N \Var \left( \frac{g^n_t}{z_t}\right) \\

            &= \frac{\exp(D_t) - 1}{N}
    \end{align}
    Given this, we can approach the overall estimator using independence under

    \begin{align}
        \E \hat Z_\SIR &= \E \prod_{t=1}^T R_t \\
        &= \prod_{t=1}^T \E R_t \\
        &= \prod_{t=1}^T z_t \\
        &= \frac{Z_T}{Z_0} \\
        &= Z
    \end{align},
    again assuming a normalized reference and ELE.
    \begin{align}
        \E\left( \frac{\hat Z_\SIR}{Z} \right)^2 &= 
            \prod_{t=1}^T \left( \Var \left( \frac{\hat R_t}{z_t} \right) + 1 \right) \\
            &= \prod_{t=1}^T \left(
               \frac{\exp(D_t) - 1}{N} + 1
            \right)
    \end{align}.
    Which gives the desired result
    \begin{align}
        \Var\left(\frac{\hat Z_\SIR}{Z} \right) &= 
            \prod_{t=1}^T \left(
               \frac{\exp(D_t) - 1}{N} + 1
            \right) - 1 
    \end{align}.

    And hence building on previous results
    \begin{align}

            \frac 1 N \int_{0}^{1} I(\varphi(u))\dot\varphi(u)^2 \d u + o(N^{-1}T^{-1})
    \end{align}
    Which in the limiting case with fixed $N$ gives
    \[
        \lim_{T \to \infty} T\Var \left[\frac{\hat Z_\AIS}{Z}\right] = 
            \frac 1 N \int_{0}^{1} I(\varphi(u))\dot\varphi(u)^2 \d u = 
            \frac{E[\varphi]}{N}

    For SIR the product can be turned into a sum using logarithms hence
    \begin{align}
        \prod_{t=1}^T \left(1 + \frac{\exp(D_t)-1}{N}\right) - 1

            \log\left(1 + \frac{\exp(D_t)-1}{N} \right)

    Using our above small-scale approximations we get

        \exp(D_t) - 1 =  \dot\varphi\left(\frac t T\right)^2 I(\beta_t)T^{-2} + o(T^{-3})

    Equality is immediate. Taking $\varphi(u) = F^{-1}(\Lambda u)$ and using the
    Fundamental Theorem of Calculus we get
    \begin{align}
        F(\varphi(u)) &= \Lambda u \\
        F'(\varphi(u))\dot\varphi(u) &= \Lambda \\
        \sqrt{I(\varphi(u))}\dot\varphi(u) &= \Lambda \\
        I(\varphi(u))\dot\varphi(u)^2 &= \Lambda^2 \\
        E[\varphi] &= \int_0^1 I(\varphi(u))\dot\varphi(u)^2 \d u \\
            &= \int_0^1 \Lambda^2 \d u  \\
            &= \Lambda^2
    \end{align}
    Inequality can be shown by Jensen's. Note that using a $u$-subsitution
    \begin{align}
        \int_0^1 \sqrt{I(\varphi(u))}\dot\varphi(u) \d u &= 
            \int_0^1 \sqrt{I(\beta)}\d \beta \\
            &= \Lambda
    \end{align}
    We apply Jensen's inequality for $\psi(x)=x^2$. This is a convex function
    so we get
    \begin{align}
        \int_0^1 I(\varphi(u))\dot\varphi(u)^2 \d u &= 
            \int_0^1 \left(\sqrt{I(\varphi(u))\dot\varphi(u)}\right)^2 \d u \\
            &\geq \left[ \int_0^1 \sqrt{I(\varphi(u))\dot\varphi(u)} \d u \right]^2 \\
            &= \Lambda^2
    \end{align}

    practice. Firstly, from results in (a) we see that the \emph{importance of
    annealing}, since without annealing the difficulty of our problem is

    \emph{linearly in

    asymptotically efficient}. Finally section (c) discusses optimal annealing
    schedules for large $T$. The optimal schedule is one which has
    \emph{unit speed} in terms of Fisher-Rao distance (recalling that the Fisher

\section{Problem 2}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item % a
    Firstly we show that the linear path consists of mean shifted normals. This
    is naturally given that the normal is an exponential family distribution,
    however for completeness we list out the details. Here
    we take the linear path to be given by
    \[
        \pi_\beta(x) = \frac{1}{Z(\beta)}
        \exp\left(-\frac {1}{2\sigma^2}\left(
            (1-\beta) x^2 + \beta(x-\mu)^2
        \right)\right)
    \]
    The exponent then becomes 
    \begin{align}
        -\frac 1 {2\sigma^2}\left(
            x^2 - 2x\mu\beta + \beta\mu^2
        \right)
    \end{align}
    Which we recognize as (up to a constant in $x$) the log-likelihood of a
    normal with mean $\mu\beta$ and variance $\sigma^2$.
    We next derive the Fisher information (working now in $\mu$ again and
    not $\beta$) 
    \begin{align}
        \ell(\mu) &= \log \Norm(x; \mu, \sigma^2) \\
            &= -\frac 1 2 \log(2\pi\sigma^2) - \frac 1 2 \frac{(x-\mu)^2}{\sigma^2} \\
        \frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \mu} &=
            -\frac{(x-\mu)}{\sigma^2} \\
        \frac{\partial^2 \ell}{\partial \mu^2} &= \frac{1}{\sigma^2} \\
        I(\mu) = \E\frac{\partial^2 \ell}{\partial \mu^2} &= \frac{1}{\sigma^2}
    \end{align}
    In one dimension the path integral is just a normal integral up to a sign. 
    We get
    \begin{align}
        \Lambda &= \left| \int_{\mu_0}^{\mu_1} \sqrt{I(\mu)} \d \mu \right| \\
            &= \left| \int_{\mu_0}^{\mu_1} \frac{1}{\sigma} \d u \right| \\
            &= \frac{|\mu_1-\mu_0|}{\sigma}
    \end{align}
    \item %b 
    Similarly to the previous section we seee that the linear path corresponds
    to simply varying $\sigma$ since 
    \begin{align}
        \frac{(1-\beta)}{2\sigma_0^2} (x-\mu)^2 + \frac{\beta}{2\sigma_1^2} (x-\mu)^2 
        &= 
        \frac 1 2(x-\mu)^2\left(\frac {1-\beta}{\sigma_0^2} + \frac {\beta}{\sigma_1^2} \right)
    \end{align}
    In essence we are linearly varying the precision.
    We derive the Fisher information for $\sigma^2$ fixing $\mu$ and using that
    $\E X = \mu$
    \begin{align}
        \frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \sigma^2} &=
            -\frac 1 {2\sigma^2} + \frac 1 2 \frac{(x-\mu)^2}{\sigma^4} \\
        \frac{\partial \ell^2}{{\partial \sigma^2}^2} &=
            \frac{1}{2\sigma^4} - \frac{(x-\mu)^2}{\sigma^6} \\
        I(\sigma^2) &=
            \frac{1}{2\sigma^4}
    \end{align}
    Taking the one-dimensional integral again we find
    \begin{align}
        \int_{\sigma_0^2}^{\sigma_1^2} \sqrt{I(\sigma^2)} \d \sigma^2 
            &= \int_{\sigma_0^2}^{\sigma_1^2} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}\sigma^2} \d \sigma^2 \\
            &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left[\log \sigma_1^2 - \log \sigma_0^2 \right] \\
            &= \sqrt{2} \left[ \log \sigma_1 - \log \sigma_0 \right]
    \end{align}
    Taking the sign into account for the path integral we get the Fisher-Rao
    length of the path is
    \begin{align}
        \Lambda &= \left| \sqrt{2} \left[ \log \sigma_1 - \log \sigma_0 \right] \right| \\
            &= \sqrt{2} \left| \log \frac{\sigma_0}{\sigma_1} \right| 
    \end{align}
    \item % c 
    We can use the piecewise linear annealing path
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Move $\sigma$ to $\sigma' = |\mu_1 - \mu_0|$
        \item Increase $\mu$ from $\mu_0$ to $\mu_1$
        \item Move $\sigma$ back to it's starting point
    \end{itemize}
    Note that if $\sigma$ is already greater than $z = |\mu_1 - \mu_0|$ in 
    practice it would be better not to move it, but we use this construction for
    the theoretical analysis.
    Given the calculations in the previous section of the question, the lengths
    of each section of the path are
    \begin{itemize}
        \item $\Lambda_1 = \sqrt{2}\left|\log \frac{z}{\sigma}\right|$
        \item $\Lambda_2 = \frac{|\mu_1 - \mu_0|}{\sigma'} = 1$
        \item $\Lambda_3 = \sqrt{2}\left|\log \frac{z}{\sigma}\right|$
    \end{itemize}
    The total length is $\Lambda = \Lambda_1 + \Lambda_2 + \Lambda_3 = 2\sqrt{2}\left|\log \frac{z}{\sigma}\right| + 1$
    which is $O(\log z)$ as desired.
    \item % d
    This result is useful for annealing algorithms because the Fisher-Rao length
    specifies the difficulty of an annealing path, with higher $\Lambda$
    corresponding to a harder path. This has many realizable effects, for
    example for NRPT with a dense enough annealing schedule, the round trip rate
    is inversely proportional to lambda ($\text{RR} = \frac{1}{2 + \Lambda}$),
    and for AIS and SMC, the variance of our estimators is lower bounded by
    $\frac 1 T \Lambda^2$, as seen in question 1. Thus, if we can choose a smarter annealing
    path we can improve the maximum performance of our annealing algorithms.

    In this toy example, we see that the linear path between two
    distributions is not always the shortest path. Indeed, changing just
    $\mu$ results in an $O(z)$ path length, whereas by changing the variance
    first we can get an $O(\log z)$ total path length, an impressive improvement!

    \item % a
    We start by deriving the incremental weights for our linear path. Note that
    we let
    \begin{align}
        \log \gamma(x) &=
            -\frac 1 2 \left((1-\beta)x^2 + \beta (x - \mu) ^2 \right) 
            - \frac 1 2 \log(2\pi) \\
            &= -\frac 1 2 \left( x^2 - 2x\mu\beta + \beta\mu^2\right)
            - \frac 1 2 \log(2\pi)
    \end{align}
    Incrementing $\beta$ by $\Delta\beta$ gives weights of 
    \[
        x\mu\Delta\beta - \frac 1 2 \Delta\beta \mu^2 = 
            \Delta\beta \left(x\mu - \frac 1 2 \mu^2 \right)
    \]
    One thing to note about this formulation is that while $Z(0) = Z(1) = 1$,
    this is not actually true of the intermediary distributions due to the 
    missing $\exp\left(\beta^2 \mu^2 - \beta \mu^2\right)$ factor required to
    complete the square. However, this intermediary rescaling does not affect
    our final answer.

    Using the template code and Listing \ref{lst:ais} we run 200 iterations of
    AIS with $N=100$ particles for various numbers of intermediate
    distributions.  Results are show in Figure \ref{fig:ais}. Ribbon bands show
    $\pm$ one standard deviation of the estimates of $\log Z$.
    
    There are a few things to note about this plot. Firstly, in theory each of
    these estimators is unbiased, however, the figures do not show this for
    small $T$. This happens because the estimators also have heavy right-tails.
    Thus without very large sample sizes we are actually observing the
    \emph{modal} response. Under ELE, the modal response doesn't approach the
    true value ($0$) until around $T=\Lambda$. Furthermore, we notice that with
    poor mixing (low $k$), the estimates are much worse than the ELE case. We
    need around $k=20$ before we approach the ELE baseline.  In the worst case
    $(k=1)$ this takes until after $T = \Lambda^2$, which represents more than
    a $10 \times$ slowdown. Finally all ribbos appear to be shrinking as
    $T$ increases. This
